Introduction

Project schedule management is a subfield of project management that concerns itself with defining stories (small, dependent chunks of work that collectively constitute the project), sequencing those stories (mapping their interdependence), estimating their time requirements, planning a schedule, and controlling that schedule (putting it into practice and iteratively adjusting it).

This article should serve as a comprehensive how-to-guide for the estimation stage. Within it, we'll provide a window into the definition and sequencing stages, contextualizing them within the scope of time requirement estimation. Then, we'll explain story point assignment, covering the benefits of relative and unitless estimations. Following that, we'll delve into various “converging” activities teams can employ to agree on shared estimations. Finally, we'll explain how concrete time requirement estimations can be derived by tracking velocity and extrapolating on past progress.

Gathering Stories

To set the tone for the remainder of the article, we'll explain a bit about the differences between estimation in Waterfall and Agile contexts. Most Waterfall projects typically begin with a multi-week-long estimation phase, consisting of enumerating every project requirement, constructing detailed stories and dependency graphs, and figuring out exactly who should work on each story and for how long. Only once all of this legwork is done can a final, emerging estimation of when the project will be completed be constructed.

Although this strategy is extremely thorough, it suffers from a few notable weaknesses (Sliger):

  • It's heavy in overhead
    • Beginning new projects requires extensive planning and communication between team members and stakeholders
  • It isn't universally applicable
    • Reliable information about how long stories will take to complete or even how to deconstruct requirements into stories isn't always available
  • It's ill-adapted to change
    • Because constructing such a thorough plan is a heavy undertaking, changing requirements or circumstances inevitably invalidating it means wasted effort

One solution is to adopt a top-down, rather than bottom-up approach to story gathering. Under this paradigm, the team assembles “just enough to get started, with a plan to incorporate more knowledge as work outputs evolve, [allowing] the project team to react quickly” (Sliger).

Assigning Point Values

Once stories have been decided on, their relative implementation difficulty can be deduced, which is usually a good proxy for relative time requirement. Engineers (and no one else) assign each story a point value–traditionally a Fibonacci number:

1, 2, 3, 5, 8, 13, 21, 34, …

It's important to note that point values are unitless (i.e. they shouldn't be converted to any unit of time). The point of estimating them isn't to directly estimate the time required to complete a story; rather, they should vary by team, indicate relative difficulty and, as we'll see later, are helpful in tracking velocity (which can be used to estimate time requirements) (Sliger).

Converging on Point Values

In order to move on to the next stage in the estimation process, teams must assign point values collaboratively so they can agree on a rating for every story. Although this task might seem trivial, there are many approaches to converging on point value assignment. We summarize some of the most common techniques below.

Planning Poker

Planning poker is a toy game and convergence activity that can facilitate team discussion around story point values. In the game, each engineer is given a set of cards, each corresponding to a reasonable point value. Then, the team lead recites various stories, explaining their story statements and acceptance criteria. Engineers pick a card they feel best represents the effort required to complete the story, and, at once, reveal their cards. Then, constructive discussions can be had about why certain point values were chosen, and agreements can be made on what the “best” point value is–whether that's defined in terms of conservativeness, popular vote, a mixture of both, etc. (Preston).

Affinity Mapping

Unlike Planning Poker, affinity mapping relies less on pre-concealed, individual judgements about stories' difficulty and instead takes on a more collective form. Teams practicing affine mapping construct a continuous scale (or discrete set of buckets) encompassing varying levels of difficulty. Then, team members take turns picking stories and placing them on the scale, conversing along the way about why they made the decision they did and debating not about the stories' absolute placements, but their relative placements (Preston).

Affine mapping can be preferable for projects whose components are more amenable to comparison than absolute estimation. It can also be easier, since it relies on comparison, initially against a pre-chosen baseline that's changeable at will.

The Three-Point Method

The three-point method is another converging activity whose conclusions are usually more telling than those of the activities we've discussed so far. This is because rather than having engineers estimate a static value and discuss, the three-point method requires them to estimate three values (Guthrie):

  1. A pessimistic estimate (b)
  2. A realistic estimate (m)
  3. An optimistic estimate (a)

Once that's done, the values are combined to produce a single estimation using one of two distributions (Guthrie):

  1. The beta distribution: (b + 4m + a) / 6
  2. The triangular distribution: (b + m + a) / 3

Estimating Velocity and Time to Completion

Now that point values have been assigned and agreed upon, the team can proceed with developing an initial prototype and computing their velocity. To do the latter, the team divides the number of story points they cumulatively tackled by the amount of time they required to do so. Ideally, the time period should be long enough to produce a somewhat accurate estimate.

Then, the team extrapolates, dividing their velocity by the percentage of the project they've completed (in story points) to yield an estimate of how much more time they require. The team should regularly compute this value and adjust the rate at which they work to meet deadlines agreed upon with stakeholders.
