Agile is all about making a collaborative effort to reach a common goal. Agile teams have many responsibilities that they need to work on together, so it is important that they are working in perfect harmony. What happens when the project expands to encompass additional objectives? Project teams could conceivably become inundated with a complex array of impending deadlines and assignments. However, do not worry, as Agile visualization emerges as a solution to this predicament.

What Is Agile Visualization

Agile visualization is the process of chunking down large and complex data set into a visual format tailored for our agile team's ease of comprehension. The goal behind such visualization techniques is to foster transparency among your peers to make it easier to collaborate on a project. Efficient planning and budgeting time to visualizations is a key component in successful project outcomes. Let’s take a look at some of the visualizations we can use in Agile project management.

Gantt Charts

A Gantt chart in Agile is a graphical representation that presents a chronological timeline of the project at hand. It comprises a vertical task list on the left, which displays commencement and conclusion dates. Furthermore, it also monitors the current status of the ongoing task and identifies the individuals accountable for each specific task. The tasks are additionally depicted as rectangular bars on a timeline, with the length of each bar corresponding to the duration required for the respective task. Certain Gantt charts will also feature special icons such as a diamond, to track significant project milestones. In order to create a Gantt chart, these general steps can be followed:

  1. Generate individual tasks for each user story within the project and append subtasks for more extensive narratives. Ensure comprehensive task descriptions are provided to equip every team member with the requisite information.
  2. Establish a sequential start-to-finish dependency for each task within your iteration, where Task B is prohibited from commencing until Task A has reached completion. Additionally, consider associating milestones with these dependencies to maintain project adherence to the schedule.
  3. Outline the roles and responsibilities within the project. Confirm the availability of resources and the readiness of team members to take on new tasks. Then, proceed to assign individuals to their respective roles.
  4. Monitor the progress of Agile teams by conducting daily standup meetings to evaluate the advancement of each iteration. Utilize these meetings to review the status of Gantt chart progress bars and make any necessary adjustments.
  5. After implementing the Gantt chart, allocate time to engage with your team. Confirm their proficiency in utilizing the Gantt chart software and schedule supplementary training sessions as required.

Burndown Charts

Burndown charts visually depict the pace of your team's work, mapping user stories over time. This chart adopts an end-user viewpoint, updating only when a user story is successfully accomplished. Additionally, the burndown chart serves as a record of the team's rate, referred to as velocity, and aids in forecasting their future performance. The burndown chart comprises multiple elements. Its axes are dedicated to representing time or iterations (horizontal) and user story points (vertical). The initial stage of a project is depicted at the far left, also marking the peak point of an ideal burndown chart. Conversely, the graph's far right signifies the conclusion of your project or sprint. In numerous burndown charts, an "ideal work remaining" line is often displayed, typically as a dotted line or in a distinct color. This line leverages the team's historical performance as a basis for projecting their future performance, serving as a benchmark for evaluating current progress.

Kanban Boards

A Kanban board represents an agile project management instrument meticulously crafted to facilitate the visualization of tasks, constraint of work-in-progress, and optimization of efficiency or workflow. It proves valuable for both agile and DevOps teams in instilling structure into their daily operations. Employing cards, columns, and an ethos of continuous improvement, Kanban boards enable technology and service teams to ensure a balanced workload and successful task completion. Kanban boards can be dissected into five fundamental components: visual signals, columns, work-in-progress limits, a commitment point, and a delivery point. Let’s take an in depth look at each of these components:

  1. Visual Signals - Kanban teams transcribe their projects and work items onto individual cards, typically dedicating one card per item. In agile teams, each card might represent a single user story. Once these cards are placed on the board, they serve as visual cues that enable team members and stakeholders to swiftly grasp the team's ongoing work and priorities.
  2. Columns - Another defining characteristic of a Kanban board is its arrangement into columns. Each column signifies a distinct task or stage, collectively forming a workflow. Cards move through this workflow, progressing from one column to the next until they are finished.
  3. Work in Progress (WIP) - WIP limits define the upper threshold of cards allowed within a single column at any given moment. For example, a column with a WIP limit of three can accommodate no more than three cards simultaneously. When a column reaches its WIP limit, it signals that the team must focus on those cards, collectively addressing them to advance them through the workflow before new cards can enter that particular stage. These WIP limits play a pivotal role in identifying bottlenecks within the workflow and optimizing the overall flow.
  4. Commitment Point - In the context of Kanban boards, teams frequently maintain a backlog where customers and team members can propose project ideas for future consideration. The commitment point marks the juncture at which a team decides to take up an idea from the backlog and commence work on the corresponding project. It signifies the initiation of the project's execution phase.
  5. Delivery point - the delivery point marks the culmination of a team's workflow. Typically, this point is reached when the product or service is successfully delivered into the hands of the customer. The primary objective of the team is to expedite the journey of cards from the commitment point to the delivery point, aiming to minimize the time elapsed between these two milestones, a metric known as Lead Time.
